Method 25A and Method 18

Both methods evaluate gaseous organic emissions, but they answer different questions. Method 25A measures total organic concentration as one combined response. Method 18 separates and identifies individual organic compounds by gas chromatography.

Method 25A

A total hydrocarbon method that uses a flame ionization detector (FID) to continuously measure total gaseous organic concentration from a source sample.

  • Best for total organic concentration trends
  • Commonly reported as propane equivalents or carbon equivalents
  • Uses heated sample transport to reduce condensation risk

Method 18

A compound-specific method that uses gas chromatography to separate, identify, and quantify gaseous organic compounds in emissions.

  • Best for speciated VOC analysis
  • Uses retention time and calibration standards for identification
  • Can use FID, PID, ECD, or other appropriate detectors

Choosing a Method

Choose Method 25A when the key question is the total hydrocarbon involved in a sample stream.

Choose Method 18 when the composition matters and individual compounds must be separated, identified, and quantified.

If the correct method is not clear, VIG can help review the monitoring objective, target compounds, sample conditions, and reporting needs to recommend a practical equipment and service path.

Method 25A: Total Gaseous Organic Concentration by Flame Ionization

EPA Method 25A is a reference test method for determining total gaseous organic concentration using a flame ionization detector. It is useful when the measurement question is not which individual compounds are present, but what total organic concentration exists in the gas stream.

Source GasSample is extracted from the stack or process stream.
Heated TransportProbe, filter, and heated sample line help prevent condensation.
FID AnalyzerOrganic compounds are ionized in a hydrogen flame.
Total ResponseResults are reported as calibration-gas or carbon equivalents.

What the Method Measures

Method 25A measures total organic compounds as a bulk response. The method applies to vapors consisting primarily of alkanes, alkenes, and aromatic hydrocarbons. Because the FID responds to organic carbon, the final result is normally expressed as the equivalent concentration of a calibration gas such as propane, or as carbon equivalents.

How Flame Ionization Works

A flame ionization detector burns the sample in a hydrogen-air flame. Organic compounds entering the flame form ions, and the analyzer converts the resulting ion current into a concentration signal. The stronger the organic carbon response, the higher the indicated total hydrocarbon concentration.

Sampling System Requirements

The method depends on transporting the gas sample to the analyzer without losing organic material along the way. A typical Method 25A system includes a sample probe, heated sample line, particulate filter, calibration gas routing, fuel gas, combustion air, and a flame ionization detector-based analyzer capable of meeting method performance requirements.

Calibration and Data Quality

  • Zero and calibration gases establish the analyzer response before measurement.
  • Calibration error checks verify that analyzer response agrees with known standards.
  • Drift checks evaluate whether the measurement system remains stable over the test period.
  • Results are interpreted as total organic concentration, not as a list of individual compounds.
Practical takeaway: Method 25A is the right starting point when a facility needs a continuous, total-organic measurement and does not need compound-by-compound speciation.

Method 18: Gaseous Organic Compounds by Gas Chromatography

EPA Method 18 is used to measure gaseous organic compound emissions by gas chromatography. Where Method 25A treats the sample as a total organic signal, Method 18 separates the gas mixture into individual compounds so each target compound can be identified and quantified.

What the Method Measures

Method 18 is designed for gaseous organics emitted from industrial sources. It is commonly used when the identity of individual volatile organic compounds matters, such as methane, ethane, or other target VOCs. Instead of reporting one total concentration, Method 18 can report concentrations for specific compounds after chromatographic separation.

How Gas Chromatography Works

In gas chromatography (GC), a sample is vaporized and introduced into a carrier gas, which transports it through a column containing a stationary phase. Different compounds interact differently with the stationary phase, causing them to travel at different rates and resulting in separation. The time between sample injection and detection of a compound is called its retention time. By comparing retention times to those of known standards under identical conditions, an analyst can tentatively identify the separated compounds.

Quantification and Detector Choice

After separation, each compound is detected and quantified. Method 18 allows appropriate detector principles, including flame ionization, photoionization, electron capture, or other detectors suited to the compounds of interest. Calibration standards are prepared or purchased for target compounds, and the GC is calibrated under conditions matching sample analysis.

Planning a Method 18 Test

  • The analyst should know or presurvey the likely organic compounds before final analysis.
  • Calibration standards must match the compounds and concentration ranges of interest.
  • Column and detector selection must provide enough separation to avoid resolution interference.
  • Moisture, particulate matter, and high concentrations must be managed to protect data quality.
Practical takeaway: Method 18 is the better choice when the question is compound-specific, especially when methane, ethane, non-methane organics, or other VOCs must be reported separately.

Model 200 Hydrocarbon Analyzer

FID Dual Channel, Methane / Non-Methane / Total, Oven Heated Hydrocarbon Analyzer

The Model 200 is a microprocessor-based, oven-heated methane, non-methane, and total hydrocarbon gas analyzer designed for accurate and stable measurement.

Two independent flame ionization detectors are used: one provides a continuous real-time total hydrocarbon reading, while the second works with a gas chromatography column to separate methane from non-methane components.

The sample is moved through an internal heated pump. A portion of the sample is trapped, separated through the GC column, measured for methane, and then backflushed through the column to obtain the non-methane component. Detectors, sample lines, capillaries, valves, the GC column, and related sample-contact components are maintained in a temperature-controlled oven.

Best fit: Applications that require real-time THC plus separate methane and non-methane hydrocarbon readings.

Peripheral

Liquid / Gas Separators

VIG manufactures its own liquid / gas separator for sample conditioning applications where liquid must be removed before the sample reaches the analyzer.

The separator uses a two-piece 316 stainless steel housing, a Teflon membrane, pre-filtration at the inlet, and 1/8 inch FNPT connections at the outlet and drain.

The membrane allows gases to pass while preventing liquid from passing through. Separated liquid falls into an external collection container that can be emptied as needed.

Principle of Operation

A cooling coil lowers the incoming sample gas temperature to room temperature, converting liquids in the gas phase into liquid phase. The cooled sample enters the separator, where the porous Teflon membrane allows gas to pass through while blocking liquid due to surface tension. The dry gas exits the separator for analysis.

Service note: This peripheral helps protect analyzer components in applications where moisture or condensable liquids are present.
